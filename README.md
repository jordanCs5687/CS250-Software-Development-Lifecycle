# CS 250 Portfolio: Software Development Lifecycle

This repository contains my Sprint Review and Retrospective deliverable from the CS 250 final project, built around the SNHU Travel case study. It pulls together the Agile and Scrum practices I applied across the course, where I rotated through the Scrum Master, Product Owner, Developer, and Tester roles. This artifact is part of my Computer Science program portfolio.

## Interpreting User Needs and the Role of User Stories

Interpreting user needs starts with listening to what the customer actually wants and separating that from how I might assume it should be built. In the SNHU Travel project I worked from a focus group transcript as the Product Owner, which meant pulling real requirements out of messy, conversational feedback rather than a clean spec. The job was to translate vague wants into concrete, buildable features.

User stories are what make that translation work. Writing a need in the form of "as a user, I want X so that Y" forces me to capture who the feature is for, what they want, and why it matters, all in one line. The "so that" is the part I have come to value most, because it keeps the purpose attached to the work and stops me from building something technically complete but useless to the person who asked for it. Pairing each story with acceptance criteria then turns the need into measurable pass or fail conditions, which is what let me build features like the Top Five Destination List and the vacation-type filter against a clear definition of done instead of guessing.

## How I Approach Developing Programs

My approach is iterative rather than trying to plan everything up front. Working in sprints across this course showed me that building in small, reviewable increments surfaces problems early, when they are cheap to fix, instead of at the end when they are expensive. I would rather ship a small working piece, get feedback, and adjust than disappear for weeks and hope I built the right thing.

The Agile processes I most want to carry into future work are the sprint review and the retrospective, which is exactly why this deliverable is the one I chose. The review keeps the work honest by putting it in front of stakeholders regularly, and the retrospective is where the team actually improves, by naming what went well and what to change next time rather than repeating the same mistakes. I also want to keep using user stories with acceptance criteria as my default way of scoping work, since that habit kept my development grounded in real user value throughout the project.

## What It Means to Be a Good Team Member

Rotating through all four Scrum roles taught me that being a good team member is mostly about communication and accountability. A good teammate makes their work visible, raises blockers early instead of hiding them, and writes things down so the next person can pick up where they left off. As Scrum Master I saw how much smoother things run when people are honest in standups, and as a developer I felt how much it costs when they are not.

It also means respecting the other roles. The Product Owner, developers, and testers all see the product differently, and a good team member treats those perspectives as checks that make the product better rather than obstacles. Reliability matters too. Doing what you committed to in the sprint, and flagging it honestly when you cannot, is what lets a team actually trust its own plan.
